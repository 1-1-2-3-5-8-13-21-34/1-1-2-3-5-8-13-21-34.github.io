Spectacled Caiman

Species Overview & Physical Characteristics

The Spectacled Caiman, also known as the white caiman, common caiman, and speckled caiman, is a crocodillian reptile in the family Alligatoridae. They are typically brownish-green or yellowish-gray colored, and have "Spectacle"-like ridge between their eyes, which is the origin of their common name. They typically grow to reach lengths of 1.4-2.5 meters, or 4.5-8.2 feet, and can reach of weights of 7-40 kilograms, or 15-88 pounds, with males being both longer and heavier than females.

Discovery & Early History

The spectacled caiman was origninally described Carl Linnaeus in 1758, as "Lacerta Crocodilus." It has since been redescribed several times, including as "Caiman sclerops" by Schneider in 1801. Although Caiman crocodilus is now the scientific name of the species, some scientists still prefer using sclerops, as having crocodilus as the scientific name for a caiman may cause confusion.


Genealogy & Classification

The spectacled caiman is one of three extant (living) species of the genus Caiman, the other two being the Yacare caiman (Caiman yacare) and the Broad-snouted caiman (Caiman latirostris). There are also several extinct fossil species in the genus Caiman, possibly up ton eight species. The spectacled caiman is a member of the caiman subfamily Caimaninae, and is one of six living species of caiman. Habitat & Environment

The spectacled caiman has the largest range of any caiman, and of any New World crocodilian. It is found in various countries throughout the Americas. It lives in Brazil, Colombia, Costa Rica, Ecuador, El Salvador, French Guiana, Guatemala, Guyana, Honduras, Mexico, Nicaragua, Panama, Peru, Suriname, Trinidad and Tobago, and Venezuela, and may also be extant in Belize and Bolivia. It has been introduced to Isla de la Juventud in Cuba, Puerto Rico, and Florida in the United States; in the latter, it is sometimes mislabeled as the American alligator (Alligator mississippiensis). Invasive populations have become established in South Florida, with isolated records further north in the state. Genetic study indicates two separate introduction events into Florida. It is intolerant to cold climates, so its range is unlikely to expand to further north than Florida. It usually lives in forests, inland bodies of fresh water (such as wetlands and rivers), grasslands, shrublands, and savannas, but is very adaptable. It prefers habitats with calm water containing floating vegetation, usually flooding and drying seasonally. It is most common in low-lying areas, but has been found at elevations of up to 800 m (2,600 ft). In Brazil, the species lives in the rivers Amazon, Araguaia, Araguari, Itapicuru, Rio Negro, Paranaíba, Solimões, Tapajós, Tocantins, and Xingu. It is able to live in human-inhabited areas.
